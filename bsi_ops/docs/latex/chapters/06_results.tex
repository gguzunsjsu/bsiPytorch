\chapter{Results}

\section{Why the GPU Results Should Be Read Together with the Earlier CPU Study}
The current GPU results make the most sense when they are read together with the earlier CPU observations. The CPU work established that BSI could represent vectors compactly and support dot product, summation, and multiplication directly on slices, but that those operations were not yet competitive in execution time on a CPU. The GPU work therefore should not be read as an isolated kernel study. It is the second stage of a longer argument. The first stage showed that the data structure was expressive and memory-conscious; the second stage asks whether the same representation can be executed materially better on massively parallel hardware.

That framing matters because it clarifies what success means. Success in the present study does not require BSI to beat dense FP16 in every case. Success means that the system provides a working end-to-end transformer inference path, preserves meaningful compression, and improves same-bit execution enough to make the remaining bottlenecks explicit and analyzable.

Several patterns are immediately visible.

First, BSI produces substantial compression under \texttt{scope=all}. The reduction is modest in absolute terms for \texttt{opt-125m}, but becomes much more significant as model size grows. By \texttt{opt-6.7b}, the static representation is reduced from roughly 12.7~GB to just above 5.0~GB.

Second, \texttt{scope=attention} preserves a much larger fraction of the dense model footprint because fewer layers are replaced. This is expected, but it is important because the attention-only regime later emerges as the better quality-preserving operating point.

Third, the BSI dot path scales less favorably than dense Torch as the model width grows. For \texttt{opt-125m}, BSI under \texttt{scope=all} is already competitive enough to be interesting. By \texttt{opt-6.7b}, the gap between native BSI dot time and dense FP16 dot time is large even though the storage savings are substantial. This is the central tradeoff of the study.

\begin{tabular}{lrrrr}
\toprule
Model & Dense Top-1 & BSI Top-1 & Dense Top-5 & BSI Top-5 \\
\midrule
OPT-125M & 0.605 & 0.626 & 0.725 & 0.729 \\
OPT-1.3B & 0.722 & 0.653 & 0.873 & 0.796 \\
OPT-6.7B & 0.798 & 0.741 & 0.929 & 0.896 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The smallest model is relatively forgiving. In that regime, the fixed-bit BSI replacement is close enough to the dense baseline that the memory savings are compelling. The gap widens at \texttt{opt-1.3b} and widens further at \texttt{opt-6.7b}. This behavior is consistent with a uniform fixed-bit policy that becomes too blunt as the model grows in width and depth. The result does not invalidate BSI as a representation. It shows that the fixed same-bit regime is usable but not yet universally robust.

\section{Case Study: \texttt{opt-6.7b}}
The \texttt{opt-6.7b} model is the most informative single checkpoint in the study because it is large enough for the kernel bottleneck to be unmistakable, but still small enough to run repeatedly for design iteration.

Under the primary evaluation setting, the dense FP16 baseline produces top-1 accuracy of 0.798, top-5 accuracy of 0.929, and per-sample dot latency of 15.493~ms. Under the BSI same-bit baseline with \texttt{scope=all}, the model size drops to 5022.281~MB, top-1 accuracy becomes 0.741, top-5 accuracy becomes 0.896, and per-sample dot latency rises to 66.452~ms. Under \texttt{scope=attention}, the static size is 10141.031~MB, top-1 accuracy is 0.779, top-5 accuracy is 0.921, and dot latency is 23.003~ms.

This single case captures the main tradeoff of the thesis. The more aggressively BSI is applied, the more compression it yields. At the same time, that broader replacement increases the cost of BSI dot execution and exposes more of the model to quantization error. Attention-only replacement gives up a portion of the compression benefit, but it preserves far more of the dense baseline behavior. This is not a nuisance result; it is a systems result that reveals where the present BSI operating point is strongest.

\section{Kernel-Only Interpretation}
Kernel-only experiments were used to study the pure dot kernel without full-model overhead. The most important shapes were the FC1-like \(Q=512, R=16384, D=4096\) case and the very-wide \(Q=512, R=50272, D=4096\) case. These measurements showed that kernel changes can materially alter dot latency even when they do not change the numerical representation. However, they also showed that kernel-only wins are not automatically end-to-end wins. Any design that improves the raw kernel but increases runtime query construction or duplicates storage may still regress in full model execution.

The thesis therefore treats kernel-only results as necessary but not sufficient evidence. The final acceptance criterion for the design remains the end-to-end model run.

\section{What the Results Prove}
Taken together, the measured results support four concrete statements.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=2em]
\item BSI provides real compression across all three primary OPT models.
\item A same-bit BSI inference path can be integrated into end-to-end transformer inference without breaking the model interface.
\item The speed and quality tradeoff depends strongly on replacement scope, with attention-only replacement being much more robust than full replacement.
\item The remaining gap to dense FP16 inference is not a vague limitation; it is now localized to the execution cost of the BSI dot path and the way that path scales with model width.
\end{enumerate}

Those are strong enough to support the thesis claim without overstating what the current implementation achieves.
